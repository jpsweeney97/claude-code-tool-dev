# Cover Letter Reference

A cover letter does one job: make the hiring manager's decision easier. It answers:

- **Why you?** (capability + proof)
- **Why this role/company now?** (intent + fit)
- **Can you communicate like a real human?** (clarity + voice)

Cover letters matter most when they function as a writing sample and context layer — not a resume rehash. They add the most value when the role treats them as part of the screening process or as a writing-skill signal.

---

## Decision: Write a Full Letter, Short Note, or Skip?

Cover letters are most worth it when they provide information the resume can't.

| Condition | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Applicant is not an obvious match (career change, different industry) | Full letter — explain the connection explicitly |
| Need to explain why this specific role, not "any job" | Full letter |
| Have a referral or prior conversation | Full letter — reference the connection |
| Role values writing, client communication, remote collaboration, or leadership | Full letter — the letter itself is proof |
| Need to address something delicate (gap, relocation, unusual path) | Full letter — address briefly and move on |
| Cover letter is optional and applicant is applying at scale | Short application note (8-12 lines) |
| No mechanism to submit a cover letter | Skip |

Default: write a full letter.

---

## What Hiring Managers Screen For

A cover letter should read as a credible, specific, low-drama note that demonstrates:

1. **Relevance, fast** — within the first few lines: the exact role, a one-sentence fit thesis, and the most relevant evidence. Start from the employer's side: what problem are they solving, what skills are they seeking?

2. **Proof over claims** — examples, outcomes, and constraints (what you did, how, what happened). Be brief but specific. Use action words, not flowery language.

3. **Real tailoring** — demonstrate understanding of the role's priorities, the team's environment (scale, tooling, customer type, risk), and what success likely means in the first 3-6 months. Generic mission praise is not tailoring.

4. **Authentic voice** — AI-generated content fails when it's polished but generic — "grammatically correct and emotionally vacant." The differentiator is specificity, honest voice, and accurate detail.

5. **Skills evidence** — the letter should read like evidence of demonstrated competencies, not a biography.

## Structure: Hook → Proof → Fit → Close

### Paragraph 1: Hook + Fit Thesis

State immediately:

- The exact role title and how the applicant found it (or name the referral)
- One-sentence fit thesis: relevant identity + core strength + value to this team
- One proof hint: a metric, scale, outcome, or domain credential

The reader must know within 3 sentences: what role, what the applicant offers, and one reason to believe it.

### Paragraph 2: Proof Story (Most Relevant)

Select **one** capability from the job posting. Construct a mini-story:

| Element | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Context | What problem existed |
| Action | What the applicant did |
| Outcome | Measurable impact |
| Transfer | Why this maps to the target role |

### Paragraph 3 (Optional): Second Proof + Company Fit

Include only if it adds net-new information:

- A second proof story covering a different required skill, OR
- How the applicant's approach aligns with how this team likely operates (tools, cross-functional work, customers, risk)

Omit if it would be redundant or thin.

### Closing: Forward Motion

- Reiterate interest in the specific role
- State availability for conversation
- Thank the reader
- Optionally reference portfolio or work samples

## Tailoring Procedure

### Step 1: Extract the Employer's Problem Statement

From the job posting, write one sentence:

> "This role exists to `[objective]` by `[method/scope]`."

### Step 2: Select 2-3 Provable Requirements

Not 8. Not "everything." The best letters are **selective**.

For each, attach:

- a project
- a constraint
- an outcome metric (even directional)

### Step 3: Identify One Specific "Why Here" Detail

Acceptable sources:

- A recent product change or roadmap signal
- A specific announcement affecting the role
- The team's stated priorities
- A specific customer segment they serve

Unacceptable:

- Generic mission praise
- "Innovative, fast-paced environment"
- Copy-pasted About page language

### Step 4: Mirror Job Posting Keywords

Use the posting's terminology where it naturally fits the applicant's real experience. Enough for clarity and relevance signaling — not keyword stuffing.

## Format Constraints

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Length | 250-400 words (half page to one page) |
| Paragraphs | 3-4 |
| Font/layout | Match the resume header and fonts |
| File format | PDF (verify formatting after conversion) |
| Greeting | Specific name if known; otherwise "Dear Hiring Manager" |
| Never use | "Dear Sir/Madam", columns, icons, heavy design |

---

## Style Rules

### Tone

Professional, direct, warm, specific. Enthusiasm reads as intent + clarity, not hype.

### Transformation Rules

Replace adjective claims with evidence:

| Input (Weak) | Output (Strong) |
|--------------|-----------------|
| "I'm a strong leader" | "I led a 6-person cross-functional launch and cut onboarding time 30% by redesigning the handoff process" |
| "I'm passionate about data" | "I built a churn dashboard adopted by Sales/CS, which changed renewal prioritization and reduced surprise churn" |

### Sentence-Level Rules

- Use concrete nouns: systems, customers, workflows, constraints
- Use numbers when available
- Vary sentence length
- Use plain transitions: "Because...", "So...", "That meant..."
- Do not overuse "I" — restructure sentences to lead with the action or outcome

## Templates

### Full Letter (3-paragraph)

```
Dear [Name / Hiring Manager],

I'm applying for [Role] at [Company]. I'm a [current role/identity] who has
[core strength] and delivered [proof metric/outcome] in [domain/context].
I'm interested in this role because [1 specific reason tied to their needs],
and I'd bring [1-2 relevant strengths] to help your team [business outcome].

In my [current/recent] role at [Company], I [did X] to solve [problem]
by [how]. This resulted in [measurable outcome]. This maps directly to
[requirement in job post], especially given [constraint/scale/tooling
relevant to them].

[Optional: A second example of fit: [short proof story]. What draws me to
[Company] specifically is [non-generic "why here" tied to product/team/
mission], and I'd be excited to contribute by [what you'd do in the role].]

Thank you for your time and consideration. I'd welcome the chance to discuss
how I can help [team/company] achieve [goal].

Sincerely,
[Name]
```

### Short Application Note (when optional / applying at scale)

```
Hi [Name] — I'm applying for [Role]. I've done [X] in [context], including
[metric/result], and I'm confident I could help your team [outcome].

Two relevant examples:
- [Example 1: action → outcome]
- [Example 2: action → outcome]

I'm especially interested in [Company] because [specific reason].
Thanks for considering my application — happy to share more detail or work samples.
```

8-12 lines. Still tailored, still proof-based.

## AI Assistance Rules

When generating or refining a cover letter with AI:

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Summarize job posting into top responsibilities + skills | Copy-paste a full AI-generated letter as final output |
| Extract keywords and map them to real experience | Invent achievements, titles, tools, or metrics |
| Convert bullet points into narrative (applicant supplies the bullets) | Include sensitive personal data in prompts (phone, SSN, confidential employer info) |
| Tighten language: remove fluff, shorten sentences | Optimize for AI-detector scores (unreliable, biased against non-native writers) |
| Check tone (too stiff? too eager? too informal?) | Add facts the applicant didn't provide |

### Useful Prompts

- "Here's the job description: [paste]. List the 5 most important capabilities and the likely business outcomes this role is accountable for."
- "Here are my 6 accomplishment bullets: [paste]. Which 2 are strongest for this role and why?"
- "Rewrite this paragraph to be shorter, more concrete, and less 'corporate' — do not add any new facts: [paste]."
- "Identify cliches / generic phrases in this draft and suggest replacements that use specific nouns and outcomes: [paste]."

The goal is specific, accurate, and credible — not "undetectable."

## Anti-Patterns

| Pattern | Why It Fails |
|---------|-------------|
| Repeating the resume in prose form | Adds no information; wastes space |
| Generic "I'm excited to apply..." with no substance | Signals template, not intent |
| No metrics, outcomes, or stakes | Claims without evidence are noise |
| Wall of text (>400 words, dense paragraphs) | Won't be read; signals poor editing |
| Over-indexing on "I" | Reads self-focused, not value-focused |
| Wrong company or role name | Instant rejection |
| Invented achievements, titles, or tools | Unverifiable claims destroy credibility |
| Fancy formatting (columns, icons, graphics) | Breaks in ATS and PDF conversion |

## Quality Checklist

### 15-Second Skim Test

After 15 seconds, can a reader answer:

- What role is this for?
- What's the candidate's fit thesis?
- What proof did they show?
- Why this company?

### "Only You Could Write This" Test

Does it contain at least **3 specifics** a generic template wouldn't know?

- A real project
- A real constraint
- A real metric or outcome
- A real reason tied to this team/company

### Integrity Test

Everything in the letter is:

- True
- Defensible in an interview
- Consistent with LinkedIn/resume
